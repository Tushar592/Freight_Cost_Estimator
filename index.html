Guide & glossary

Read this once and the rest of the tool will make sense. It explains what every number means, how a landed cost is built, which figures are solid and which are estimates, and the values used behind the scenes.

What this tool is

A landed-cost estimator for buyers without a transport management system. You enter your own freight rate and cargo, and it builds the full delivered cost — freight, insurance, customs duty, import VAT, and local charges — plus a mode comparison, carbon footprint, and a printable quotation. It is an estimate to support a decision, not a binding quote. Treat the output as a well-structured first number, then confirm freight and duty with your forwarder and customs broker.

How a landed-cost number is built (in order)

1. Cargo
Every product row is summed into total weight and total volume.
2. Chargeable weight / volume
Converted to what carriers actually bill: air uses volumetric weight (volume in cm³ ÷ 6000), sea LCL uses the revenue ton (the greater of cubic metres or tonnes), and sea FCL / road work out how many containers or trucks the cargo fills.
3. Freight
Chargeable amount × your rate, or the minimum charge if that is higher. For FCL/road it is number of units × rate.
4. Insurance
Goods value × your insurance % × 1.1 (the customary “CIF + 10%” basis).
5. Customs value (CIF)
Goods + freight + insurance. This is the value duty is charged on.
6. Duty
Each product’s own duty % applied to its share of the customs value, then added up.
7. Import VAT / GST
Your VAT % applied to (customs value + duty).
8. Local charges
Origin and destination charges are added or excluded depending on the Incoterm.
9. Landed cost
The sum of the lines your Incoterm makes you responsible for. Lines the supplier covers are struck out and excluded.

How transit days are estimated

With no schedule API connected, transit is estimated as distance ÷ an effective daily pace + a fixed handling buffer, floored at a sensible minimum. The pace is deliberately below top speed because it bakes in real-world limits (driver-hour rules, port dwell, waiting for the next flight); the buffer covers loading, terminal handling and customs at both ends.

Air estimate
round(distance ÷ 4000) + 2 days, minimum 2. A 9,000 km lane ≈ 4 days. The low pace reflects that air-freight time is dominated by handling and consolidation, not flight hours.
Sea estimate
round(distance ÷ 700) + 6 days, minimum 5. A 19,000 km Asia–Europe lane ≈ 33 days, in the usual 30–40 day range.
Road estimate
round(distance ÷ 600) + 1 day, minimum 1. A 1,200 km lane ≈ 3 days.

Typing a transit number yourself overrides the estimate and is treated as a real figure. Admin lane times (once the backend is connected) replace the formula with your own door-to-door times per route.

How the carbon footprint is calculated

Manual mode (default, free): emissions = cargo weight in tonnes × distance in km × a per-mode factor. The built-in factors are Air 0.6, Sea 0.016, Road 0.1 kg CO₂e per tonne-km (DEFRA / GLEC aligned), and they are editable in Admin so you can drop in your own source.

EcoFreight mode (optional, needs backend): when switched on in Admin and the Apps Script backend is connected with an API key, the app sends origin, destination, weight and mode to EcoFreight for GLEC v3.2 / ISO 14083 leg-by-leg emissions, and falls back to the manual factors if the service is unavailable or over its monthly free quota.

Either way the carbon number is an estimate — useful for comparing modes, not a measured figure.

Sea distance in nautical miles

Sea distances are often quoted in nautical miles. In the Comparison tab’s Sea card, the “Have nautical miles?” helper converts them for you (1 NM = 1.852 km) and fills the distance field in your current unit, which then feeds both the freight and the emissions calculation.

Genuine figures vs estimates

This is the most important section. Use the genuine numbers with confidence; treat the estimates as planning figures to verify.
Genuine from your input / definite
Goods value, duty %, VAT %, your freight rate, your min charge, origin/destination charges, insurance %, the Incoterm allocation rules, the CIF customs method, and all the arithmetic that combines them. If your inputs are right, these are right.
Estimated modelled / will vary
Chargeable weight (depends on how the cargo is actually packed), the number of containers/trucks and the stacking fit (modelled from your dimensions and a packing-efficiency assumption), the carbon footprint (a generic factor × distance × weight), transit days (distance-based), and the FX rate (live, but it moves). The real carrier invoice can differ.

Glossary

Chargeable weight
The figure a carrier bills on — the greater of actual weight and a volume-based weight, so light bulky cargo isn’t under-charged.
Volumetric (dimensional) weight
Volume converted to a weight using a divisor; here 6000 cm³/kg for air.
Revenue ton (sea LCL)
Sea LCL is billed per the larger of cubic metres or tonnes (minimum 1).
LCL / FCL
Less-than-Container Load (you share a box) vs Full-Container Load (you book a whole container).
Stacking levels
How many layers of cargo can be stacked inside a container/truck (single/double/triple), which changes how much fits.
Packing efficiency
Real loads never fill 100% of a box; the tool assumes 85% usable space.
CIF
Cost, Insurance & Freight — the value (goods + freight + insurance) that customs duty is calculated on.
Incoterm
The rule that splits costs and risk between supplier and buyer (e.g. EXW, FOB, CIF, DDP). It decides which lines you actually pay.
Duty
Import tax set by the destination country, based on the product’s HS code, applied to the customs value.
Import VAT / GST
Consumption tax charged on (customs value + duty) at import.
Carbon footprint (CO₂e)
Estimated transport emissions: weight × distance × a per-mode factor.
Landed cost
The total delivered cost of getting the goods to your door under the chosen Incoterm.
Logistics uplift
How much the landed cost exceeds the bare goods value, as a %.
